# Source: Gegen Hitze – Wissenschaftler berechnen Kühlleistung eines Baumes

- **Type:** Article (science journalism)
- **Author:** Susanne Delonge
- **Outlet:** BR24 (Bayerischer Rundfunk)
- **Date:** 2024-09-28
- **Raw file:** [[raw/Gegen Hitze Wissenschaftler berechnen Kühlleistung eines Baumes]]
- **Original URL:** https://www.br.de/nachrichten/wissen/klimawandel-bringt-mehr-hitze-die-stadt-braucht-mehr-baeume,UPOPKW8

## Summary

Researchers at TU München, led by forest scientist Thomas Rötzer, have developed a methodology to precisely calculate the cooling output of individual urban trees. The method combines measurements of tree height, crown diameter, leaf density, and shadow cast. Applied to a 20-metre tall, densely leafed tree, it yields a cooling capacity of up to **40,000 kWh per year** — equivalent to roughly **€16,000 in air conditioning costs avoided**.

The article uses Munich and Nuremberg as case studies for a broader problem: both are among Germany's most heavily sealed (impervious-surface) cities, yet urban densification is accelerating tree loss. Munich's Bund Naturschutz reports a net loss of approximately 2,000 trees per year — more are felled than planted. The primary driver is underground parking construction for new housing developments, which prevents trees from rooting deeply enough to grow large. Researcher Simone Linke (Hochschule Weihenstephan-Triesdorf) emphasizes that only large, mature trees — requiring 30–40 years of growth — deliver meaningful cooling. Trees planted above underground garages cannot reach this size.

A significant policy concern is raised: Bavaria's *Modernisierungsgesetz* (modernisation law), effective from 2025, reportedly removes a clause that previously empowered cities and municipalities to prohibit surface sealing. Researchers fear this will accelerate the loss of urban green space.

## Key Claims

1. A 20m tall, densely leafed tree provides up to **40,000 kWh/year** of cooling (shade + evapotranspiration).
2. That cooling output is equivalent to **~€16,000/year** in avoided air conditioning energy costs.
3. Munich and Nuremberg are among Germany's **most impervious-surface cities**.
4. Munich has a net tree loss of **~2,000 trees/year** (Bund Naturschutz).
5. Trees need **30–40 years** of growth to reach effective cooling size.
6. Trees planted above underground parking garages **cannot root deeply enough** to reach full cooling potential.
7. Bavaria's **Modernisierungsgesetz (2025)** removes municipal power to prohibit surface sealing.
8. Tree cooling works via two mechanisms: **shade** and **evapotranspiration** (moisture transpiration).
9. Cooling potential is quantified from: tree height, crown diameter, leaf density, and shadow cast.

## Contradictions / Gaps

- No primary research paper is cited — this is science journalism summarising Rötzer's work. The underlying methodology paper should be found and ingested as a primary source.
- No LST (land surface temperature) measurements are given — the cooling figure is energy-based (kWh), not thermal (°C reduction). Relationship to UHI intensity in degrees not established here.
- Claims about Bavaria's Modernisierungsgesetz are attributed to researchers' fears, not confirmed legal analysis.
