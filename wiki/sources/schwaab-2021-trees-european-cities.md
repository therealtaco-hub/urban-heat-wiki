# Source: Schwaab et al. (2021) — Urban Trees and LST in 293 European Cities

- **Type:** Peer-reviewed research article (Nature Communications)
- **Authors:** Jonas Schwaab, Ronny Meier, Gianluca Mussetti, Sonia Seneviratne, Christine Bürgi, Edouard L. Davin
- **Institution:** Institute for Atmospheric and Climate Science, ETH Zürich
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-021-26768-w
- **Published:** 2021 (Nature Communications 12:6763)
- **Raw file:** [[raw/s41467-021-26768-w.pdf]]

## Summary

This landmark study analyses LST differences between urban trees, treeless urban green spaces, and urban fabric across 293 European cities, using 120,285 Landsat scenes combined with land cover data. It establishes continent-scale patterns in the cooling potential of urban trees and reveals a strong climate-zone dependency: Central European cities (including Germany) benefit most, while Southern European cities see reduced effectiveness due to soil moisture limitations.

The key result for this project: **in Central European cities, urban trees show 8–12 K lower LST than continuous urban fabric in summer**. Treeless green spaces (grass, parks without trees) are 2–4 times less effective than trees. Evapotranspiration (ET) is identified as the primary driver of these differences, with albedo playing a secondary role. Notably, urban trees in Central Europe cool *more* than rural forests, likely because higher urban background temperatures increase the vapour pressure deficit and drive greater transpiration.

## Key Claims

1. Urban trees show **8–12 K lower LST than urban fabric in Central Europe** in summer (includes Germany).
2. Southern Europe: only 0–4 K lower LST (soil moisture-limited ET reduces effectiveness).
3. **Treeless green spaces are 2–4× less effective** than urban trees at reducing LST.
4. ET is the primary driver of LST differences; albedo is secondary.
5. In some Southern European/Turkish cities, urban tree LST can equal or *exceed* urban fabric LST during extreme heat — due to very low ET and relatively high albedo.
6. **Urban trees in Central Europe cool more than rural forests** — likely due to higher VPD from elevated background temperatures.
7. During hot extremes, cooling effect shifts northward (Central Europe, Scandinavia, British Isles) as VPD increases with sufficient soil moisture.
8. Dataset: 120,285 Landsat scenes + LULC data across 293 European cities.

## Contradictions / Gaps

- LST (surface temperature) is not the same as air temperature — health-relevant temperature is at human height.
- Soil moisture availability is a critical moderating variable not always known at city level.
- Results are aggregated at city scale; intra-urban variation (which neighbourhoods benefit most) not addressed.
