# Source: Elmes et al. (2017) — Urban Tree Canopy Loss and LST

- **Type:** Peer-reviewed research article (ISPRS Journal of Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing)
- **Authors:** Arthur Elmes, John Rogan, Christopher Williams, Samuel Ratick, David Nowak, Deborah Martin
- **Institutions:** Clark University (Worcester, MA); USDA Forest Service Northern Research Station
- **Published:** 2017 (received Sept 2016, accepted April 2017)
- **Study city:** Worcester, Massachusetts, USA (2008–2015)
- **Raw file:** [[raw/Effects of urban tree canopy loss on land surface temperature magnitude.pdf]]

## Summary

Worcester, Massachusetts experienced large-scale urban tree canopy (UTC) loss and subsequent replanting between 2008 and 2015, providing a natural experiment to measure the causal relationship between UTC change and LST. The study combines fine-resolution (0.5 m) UTC mapping from fused LiDAR/WorldView-2 imagery with a full Landsat time series to track both monotonic LST trends and changes in seasonal temperature patterns.

The study shows that UTC loss increases both the *magnitude* and the *duration* of urban heat: areas losing tree cover see peak LST increases of 1–6°C and the summer warm period extends by up to 15 days. UTC gain provides the inverse — moderate mitigation — but the cooling from new planting is smaller in magnitude than the warming from loss, because newly planted trees take years to grow to effective size (corroborating the Rötzer/Stratopoulos finding about tree maturity time).

## Key Claims

1. **UTC loss → peak LST increase of 1–6°C** in affected areas.
2. **Summer warm period extended by up to 15 days** in UTC loss areas.
3. UTC gain provides moderate mitigation: lower monotonic trends, lower peak temperatures.
4. **UTC gain mitigation is smaller than UTC loss warming** — asymmetric relationship.
5. Largest UTC loss occurred in **residential neighbourhoods**, increasing exposure of impervious (road) and pervious (grass) surfaces.
6. Seasonal Trend Analysis (STA) used to separate magnitude and timing effects.
7. UTC mapping at **0.5 m resolution** using LiDAR + WorldView-2 image segmentation.
8. Landsat time series used for LST — all available scenes 2007–2015.

## Contradictions / Gaps

- Study city is North American (Worcester, MA) — climate and urban form differ from Würzburg.
- LST from Landsat (30m) vs. tree mapping at 0.5m introduces scale mismatch.
- Exact species composition of lost/gained UTC not analysed — species-specific cooling not addressed.
