# Source: Stratopoulos-Le Chalony (2020) — Klimabäume für die Stadt

- **Type:** Doctoral dissertation (Dr. rer. nat.)
- **Author:** Laura Myrtie Fanê Stratopoulos-Le Chalony
- **Institution:** TU München, Wissenschaftszentrum Weihenstephan (Lehrstuhl für Strategie und Management der Landschaftsentwicklung)
- **Year:** 2020 (submitted 10.02.2020, accepted 26.06.2020)
- **Committee:** Prof. Dr. Thomas Rötzer (chair), Prof. Dr.-Ing. Stephan Pauleit, Prof. Dr.-Ing. Swantje Duthweiler (Hochschule Weihenstephan-Triesdorf)
- **Raw file:** [[raw/1536689.pdf]]

## Summary

This dissertation investigates a critical but underexplored tradeoff in urban greening: the conflict between **drought tolerance** and **cooling performance** in street trees. As climate change increases drought stress in Central European cities, planners increasingly favour drought-tolerant tree species from arid habitats (*Lebensbereich 6*). However, the physiological adaptations that make these trees drought-tolerant — reduced transpiration rates, lower biomass production — are precisely the properties that reduce their cooling contribution to the urban microclimate.

Over a two-year measurement campaign at Munich's municipal tree nursery, six commonly-planted street tree species/cultivars from two habitat types were compared. The study quantifies transpiration rates, biomass production, water use efficiency, and cooling potential under varying atmospheric and soil moisture conditions, including a drought experiment.

Key finding: drought-tolerant species (*LB6*) transpire less per unit leaf area than moisture-adapted species (*LB3*), which directly implies lower evapotranspirative cooling. The tension between "climate-resilient" tree selection and "cooling-effective" tree selection is real and must be explicitly addressed in urban planting strategies.

## Key Claims

1. **Drought-tolerant trees (LB6) transpire less per leaf area than moisture-adapted trees (LB3)**: LB3 mean transpiration 0.19 kg H₂O m⁻² day⁻¹ vs. LB6 0.17 kg H₂O m⁻² day⁻¹.
2. **LB6 trees had higher water use efficiency**: 1.12× higher max xylem flux density and up to 7× higher growth.
3. **Planting drought-tolerant trees likely reduces urban cooling effectiveness** — a tradeoff not yet well reflected in urban planning guidance.
4. The two cooling mechanisms (shade, transpiration) are both positively correlated with biomass production and water use — drought-tolerant trees score lower on both.
5. Study species: LB3 (*Acer platanoides*, *Carpinus betulus* 'Fastigiata', *Tilia cordata* 'Greenspire'); LB6 (*Acer campestre*, *Ostrya carpinifolia*, *Tilia tomentosa* 'Brabant').

## Contradictions / Gaps

- Transpiration difference is modest at sufficient soil moisture; the divergence likely grows under drought conditions.
- Results are from a nursery setting — urban street conditions (compacted soils, heat, pollution) may amplify or dampen the differences.
- Long-term survival rates of LB3 vs. LB6 trees under projected climate scenarios not addressed in this study.
