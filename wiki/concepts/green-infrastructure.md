# Green Infrastructure

Nature-based interventions in urban environments — trees, parks, street vegetation, green roofs, green walls — that reduce [[wiki/concepts/urban-heat-island]] intensity through shade and [[wiki/concepts/evapotranspiration]].

## Key Facts

- Trees are the most studied and highest-impact green infrastructure element for urban cooling.
- A single 20m tall tree can deliver up to **40,000 kWh/year** of cooling (equivalent to ~€16,000/year in avoided AC costs). *(Source: [[wiki/sources/gegen-hitze-kuehlleistung-baum]])*
- Cooling effect requires mature trees: **30–40 years** of growth needed to reach effective size.
- Trees planted above underground parking garages cannot root deeply enough to reach full size. *(Source: [[wiki/sources/gegen-hitze-kuehlleistung-baum]])*
- [[wiki/entities/muenchen]] loses a net ~2,000 trees/year — more are felled than planted. *(Source: [[wiki/sources/gegen-hitze-kuehlleistung-baum]])*

## Mechanisms / How It Works

Trees cool via two distinct pathways:
1. **Shade** — the crown blocks direct solar radiation from reaching surfaces and people.
2. **Evapotranspiration** — water transpired through leaves absorbs latent heat from the air → see [[wiki/concepts/evapotranspiration]].

Cooling potential is quantified by measuring: tree height, crown diameter, leaf density, shadow cast. *(Rötzer, TU München)*

## Debates and Uncertainties

- The 40,000 kWh figure is for a single large, mature tree under ideal conditions. Average urban trees are smaller and younger — actual per-tree cooling will typically be lower.
- Trade-off: space for trees competes directly with parking (12–20 m² per space) and housing densification.
- **Drought tolerance tradeoff**: climate-adapted drought-tolerant species transpire ~11% less than moisture-adapted species, reducing cooling effectiveness. See [[wiki/concepts/tree-species-selection]].
- Green roofs and green walls have different cooling profiles than ground-level trees — see [[wiki/sources/stangl-2019-wirkungen-gruene-stadt]] for review.
- Southern Europe: tree cooling is much reduced (0–4 K vs. 8–12 K in Central Europe) due to soil moisture limited ET. *(Source: [[wiki/sources/schwaab-2021-trees-european-cities]])*
