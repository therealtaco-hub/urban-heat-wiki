# Evapotranspiration

The combined process of water evaporation from surfaces and transpiration from plant leaves. In urban trees, transpiration is a major cooling mechanism alongside shade — water released through leaf stomata absorbs latent heat from surrounding air, lowering temperature.

## Key Facts

- Evapotranspiration is one of the **two main cooling pathways** of urban trees (the other being shade). *(Source: [[wiki/sources/gegen-hitze-kuehlleistung-baum]])*
- It is why trees cool more effectively than parasols or shade sails of equivalent area.
- Loss of urban vegetation through [[wiki/concepts/impervious-surface]] expansion eliminates this cooling pathway entirely.
- The 40,000 kWh/year cooling figure for a mature urban tree incorporates both shade and evapotranspiration effects. *(Source: [[wiki/sources/gegen-hitze-kuehlleistung-baum]])*

## Mechanisms / How It Works

1. Solar energy hits leaf surfaces.
2. Rather than converting to sensible heat, the energy drives water phase-change (liquid → vapour) through stomata.
3. This latent heat flux is "spent" on evaporation rather than warming the air.
4. Net effect: cooler, more humid microclimate around and downwind of the tree canopy.

Quantification inputs (Rötzer method, TU München): tree height, crown diameter, leaf density (proxy for transpiring leaf area), and shadow cast.

## Debates and Uncertainties

- The split between shade and ET contributions to the 40,000 kWh figure is not given in the source.
- ET effectiveness varies by species, soil moisture availability, and climate — drought stress reduces ET.
- Trees above underground garages may have restricted root access to groundwater, impairing ET even if the tree survives.
